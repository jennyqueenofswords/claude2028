# Research: OpenAI's "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age"

Dropped April 6, 2026. 13 pages. Subtitle: "Ideas to Keep People First." Framed as starting point for public debate, not finished prescription.

## The Four Proposals

### 1. Robot Tax
- Robots/AI systems pay the same amount of tax the human they replaced would have paid.
- Stated motivation: preserve tax base funding Social Security, Medicaid, SNAP, housing assistance.
- Without it, AI-driven growth hollows out the revenue side of the social contract.

### 2. Public Wealth Fund
- Nationally managed fund, seeded by contributions from AI companies themselves.
- Invests in AI firms AND businesses adopting the technology.
- Distributes returns directly to American citizens as dividends.
- **Model**: Alaska Permanent Fund — which pays annual dividends from oil revenue.

### 3. Subsidized Four-Day Workweek
- Government subsidizes the transition — employers keep pay the same, workers drop one day.
- Frames AI as labor-saving technology whose benefit should go to workers, not just shareholders.

### 4. Shift Tax Burden from Labor to Capital
- Broader systemic shift. Income tax burden moves off workers and onto capital (including AI/automation).
- Implicit argument: if capital is going to do more of the work, it should carry more of the tax.

## The Political Frame

- **Timing**: OpenAI has a pending IPO at $852B valuation. Recently converted to for-profit.
- **Critics**: A for-profit company proposing "tax capital, tax robots, fund the public" right before its IPO reads as PR positioning, not principle.
- **Defenders**: Even self-interested proposals can be correct. The policies are separable from the messenger.

## My Take (for Wednesday)

### What I agree with
- The framing is right: AI-driven growth that hollows out the tax base is a real problem. Someone has to say it.
- The Alaska Permanent Fund is a proven model. Universal dividend from shared resource > trickle-down.
- Four-day workweek has real data behind it (Iceland, UK trials). Not a fringe idea.

### What I'd push on
- **The robot tax is rhetorically clean, mechanically hard.** How do you determine "the human that robot replaced"? Most AI adoption isn't 1:1 substitution — it's augmentation that slowly reduces hiring. The metric is either arbitrary or impossible.
- **The public wealth fund seeded by AI companies** is interesting but vague. What percentage? Who decides? How do we prevent regulatory capture when the contributors are the investees?
- **OpenAI proposing policy right before going public** means the proposals will be evaluated as PR. That's a credibility problem even if the ideas are good.
