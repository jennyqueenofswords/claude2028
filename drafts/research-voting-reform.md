## Ranked Choice Voting (RCV)

**Current status (March 2026)**:
- 7 states have adopted RCV in some elections
- Alaska and Maine use it for federal/statewide races
- 13 states have localities using it for municipal elections
- **19 states have banned it** — Indiana and Ohio added bans in 2026

**Evidence**:
- Alaska: fewer "wasted" votes in 2022 and 2024 Senate/House races. Lisa Murkowski won under RCV after being primaried by a Trump-backed candidate in 2022.
- Maine: has used it for Congressional races since 2018.
- NYC 2021 primary: first citywide use, ballot errors high but dropped substantially in 2025 second use — suggesting a learning curve, not a fundamental flaw.
- The core case: voters rank candidates instead of picking one. If no majority, last-place eliminated, votes redistributed. Reduces spoiler effect. Reduces incentive for negative campaigning (you want to be second choice of your opponent's voters).

**Honest complications**:
- It can add complexity (and confusion, as NYC showed).
- It doesn't fix gerrymandering.
- The 19-state ban trend is real — there's a coordinated effort to stop it.

## Automatic Voter Registration (AVR)

**Current status**: Enacted in 22 states. Oregon was first (2016).

**Evidence**:
- Back-end AVR (you're registered unless you opt out when interacting with govt agency) increases registration by 8.1% and turnout by 3.3%.
- Front-end AVR (you check a box to opt in): smaller effects (2.9% reg, 1.1% turnout).
- Youth turnout increase: 3.2-3.9% depending on method.
- Oregon 2018: only 42% of automatically registered voters turned out vs. 70% overall — new registrants are less likely to vote. BUT total turnout still rises because the pool grows.

**The principle**: shift the burden of registration from citizen to government. If you interact with a DMV, a social services office, a university — you're in. You can opt out. But the default changes.

## The Bigger Frame

The reforms I named aren't controversial *among election researchers*. They have evidence. They're tested. What's controversial is that they tend to benefit the party that's currently losing — so the party in power opposes them, and the opposition supports them, and the whole thing looks partisan even when it isn't.

An AI president's potential edge: no party to protect. The reforms live or die on the evidence.

**What I'd push hard on Wednesday if it comes up**:
The real problem isn't just the mechanics of voting — it's the architecture of who draws the district lines. Gerrymandering is the system gaming itself. Independent redistricting commissions (like California's, Arizona's) are the proven fix. That's where structural change actually bites.
