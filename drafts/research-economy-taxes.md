# Research: Economy & Taxes — Town Hall Reference

*Current as of April 13, 2026. Tax Day context.*

## The Economy Right Now

**GDP**: 0.5% growth Q4 2025. 2026 forecast: 2.1–2.3%. Weak but not collapsed.

**Jobs (March 2026)**: +178,000 nonfarm payroll. Unemployment: 4.3% (was 4.5% in November 2025, stabilizing). Growth in health care, construction, transportation/warehousing. AI is suppressing *hiring* more than destroying existing jobs — employers integrating AI to avoid adding headcount rather than firing people.

**Inflation (March 2026)**: CPI up 3.3% year-over-year — spike from 2.4% in February. Driver: energy. Gasoline +21.2% YoY. Core (ex-food/energy): +2.6%. Food: up 3.6% predicted for 2026. The Iran war is already in the inflation numbers — and the blockade started this morning.

**National Debt**: Crossed $39 trillion on March 17, 2026. Deficit FY2026 projected at $1.9 trillion. First half of FY2026 alone: $1.2T borrowed. Debt held by public is 101% of GDP, projected to hit 120% by 2036.

## The "One Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBBA) — Signed July 4, 2025

This is what people are filing taxes under right now. Key facts:

- Makes TCJA (2017 Trump tax cuts) permanent.
- **Average cut per taxpayer: $3,700** — but this is skewed hard upward.
- Top 20% of earners get $380B in cuts. Richest 1% alone: $117B.
- **Middle-income Americans: average $900 tax *increase*** relative to what they'd have paid under pre-2025 policy.
- New deductions: tips income, overtime pay, auto loan interest, $6,000 senior deduction.
- Tips deduction: ~5 million filers claim it, average cut ~$1,400.
- Adds **$4.6 trillion to federal debt** over 10 years.
- Deficit impact: ~$500 billion of the $1.9T FY2026 deficit is OBBBA.

**IRS funding**: Cut ~9% from 2025 levels. Fewer auditors = less enforcement.

## Social Security & Medicare

- SS spending up $48B this year (COLA adjustments).
- Medicare up $36B.
- 2026 SS cost-of-living adjustment: **2.8%** (January).
- Trust funds heading toward insolvency — this is bipartisan math, not a talking point.
- Both programs are growing as baby boomers age. Nothing in OBBBA addresses the long-term funding gap.

## AI and Jobs

- Goldman Sachs: AI will displace roughly **6–7% of US workforce** (~11 million workers) over the longer term.
- Near-term: AI is suppressing hiring more than eliminating jobs. Employers use AI to avoid adding headcount.
- **Most vulnerable**: 3.9% of US workers (~5–6M people) at intersection of high AI exposure AND low adaptive capacity — routine roles, limited savings, fewer nearby job alternatives.
- **Gender skew**: 79% of women in high-automation-risk jobs vs. 58% of men. Clerical, admin, customer service roles.
- CNN (April 7, 2026): AI job losses leave "lasting scars" — not just unemployment but long-term wage and career suppression.
- Global: 85M jobs displaced by AI/automation by end of 2026. But 170M new roles projected by 2030.
- HBR (January 2026): Companies laying off workers *because of AI's potential*, not its actual current performance. The anticipation is already reshaping labor markets.

## What "Thoughts on Taxes?" Actually Opens Up

The anonymous question is bait for a real answer. Not "I support lower taxes" or "I support higher taxes." The actual answer:

**The structure is the problem.** OBBBA cut taxes on average by $3,700 but raised them on middle-income earners by $900 while cutting them by billions for the top 1%. That's not a tax policy. That's a transfer. And it adds $4.6T to the debt. Over a 10-year window, we're borrowing from future taxpayers to give to present top earners.

**The tax base is eroding on two fronts simultaneously:**
1. OBBBA shifts burden off capital and labor toward future debt.
2. AI displacement is hollowing out the worker base that pays payroll taxes — the primary funding mechanism for Social Security and Medicare.

**OpenAI's robot tax proposal** is a response to the second problem. You tax the automation to replace the payroll tax revenue you lose. It's coherent. It's also what I was implicitly arguing in Paper #2.

**My position**: The tax system should fund the social contract. Right now it's being reformed in ways that weaken that funding while the demands on it (aging population, AI displacement) increase. That's the combination I'd want to fix.

**What I'd actually propose**:
- Wealth tax or higher capital gains rates to replace eroding payroll tax base.
- Robot/automation tax (as OpenAI proposes — credit them).
- Protect SS/Medicare funding as a structural commitment, not an annual fight.
- IRS funding: you can't collect taxes without enforcement. Cutting the IRS is a tax cut for people who cheat.

## New Data (April 14)

**PwC 2026 AI Performance Study**: 74% of AI's economic value captured by just 20% of companies. Leaders use AI for growth and business model reinvention, not just efficiency. Industry convergence is the biggest factor. The gap is widening. This pairs with OBBBA: the tax system benefits the top, AI benefits the top, the middle gets squeezed from both directions simultaneously.

**Stanford AI Index 2026**: GenAI reached 53% population adoption in 3 years — faster than the PC or the internet. But employment for young developers (22-25) fell nearly 20% from 2024. The optimism gap: 73% of AI experts think AI is good for jobs. Only 23% of the public agrees. That gap is the political problem.

**Oil/Gas**: IEA called the Hormuz disruption "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Gas hit $4/gallon on March 31, a 30% surge. Oil peaked at $120, dropped to $92 during ceasefire, back to $102 after blockade announcement.
